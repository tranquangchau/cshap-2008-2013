How to Calculate Earned Value

Determine the Actual Cost of Work PerformedCalculate the Budgeted Cost of Work ScheduledCalculate the Schedule Variance and Schedule Performance Index

Edited by IngeborgK, HarryPotterKeeper, Sarah Eliza, Nicole_cuhh and 1 other

Earned Value Analysis is a proven method for accurately gauging the financial status of a project. In addition, this methodology is an effective means for projecting the total cost of a project at completion.

Ad

Steps

Method 1 of 7

  1. 1
    Prepare a project schedule. To take advantage of the power of Earned Value Analysis, your schedule must define when each project task should be done and how much each task should cost.
    Ad
  2. 2
    List the tasks required to complete the project.
  3. 3
    Identify the resources required to perform each task. Include labor and materials.
  4. 4
    Determine the amount of each resource that will be required for each task.
  5. 5
    Ascertain the unit cost for each resource which, for labor, will be an hourly rate.
  6. 6
    Determine the expected cost to perform each task.
    • Multiply the hourly rate for each required labor resource by the number of hours required.
    • Total this product for all required labor resources.
    • Calculate the total cost of materials required to complete the task.
    • Add any additional costs for such items as equipment rental, insurance, transportation, government fees, etc.
    • The total is the budgeted cost for the task.
  7. 7
    Estimate the length of time each task will take. This is the elapsed time to complete the task, not the amount of labor (applied time) needed to complete the task.
  8. 8
    Identify the prerequisites for each task. The prerequisites are the tasks that must be completed before the given task can be started.
  9. 9
    Use project scheduling software or manually determine start and finish dates for each task. Spreadsheet software is often used for small projects.

Method 2 of 7: Determine the Actual Cost of Work Performed

  1. 1
    Select the "project status date."
  2. 2
    Determine the actual costs accrued on the project up through the project status date. The total is referred to as the "Actual Cost of Work Performed" (ACWP).

Method 3 of 7: Calculate the Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled

  1. 1
    Identify the tasks scheduled to be completed on or before the project status date. Calculate the total budgeted cost of these tasks.
  2. 2
    List the tasks scheduled to start before the project status date, but not scheduled to finish before the project status date. These are your scheduled work-in-progress (WIP) tasks. Determine the percentage of each scheduled WIP task that should be completed by the project status date. Multiply the total budgeted cost by this percentage for each of these tasks.
  3. 3
    Add the calculated partial costs of the scheduled WIP tasks to the total cost of the tasks scheduled to be completed. This is the total Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled (BCWS).

Method 4 of 7

  1. 1
    Compute the total budgeted cost of the tasks that have actually been completed.
  2. 2
    Identify tasks that have been started, but not yet completed. Estimate the percent completed for each of these tasks and multiply the budgeted cost of each task by the percentage completed.
  3. 3
    Add the computed totals for the partially completed tasks to the budgeted costs of the completed tasks. The total is the Budgeted Cost of Work Performed (BCWP).

Method 5 of 7: Calculate the Schedule Variance and Schedule Performance Index

  1. 1
    Subtract the Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled from the Budgeted Cost of Work Performed to determine the Schedule Variance (SV).
    • SV = BCWP � BCWS
    • A positive Schedule Variance indicates that the project is ahead of schedule.
  2. 2
    Divide the Budgeted Cost of Work Performed by the Budgeted Cost of Work Scheduled to compute the Schedule Performance Index (SPI).
    • SPI = BCWP / BCWS
    • If the SPI is greater than 1, the project is ahead of schedule. If it is less than 1, the project is behind schedule.

Method 6 of 7

  1. 1
    Subtract the Actual Cost of Work Performed from the Budgeted Cost of Work Performed to determine the Cost Variance (CV).
    • CV = BCWP � ACWP
    • A positive Cost Variance indicates that the project is under budget.
  2. 2
    Divide the Budgeted Cost of Work Performed by the Actual Cost of Work Performed to compute the Cost Performance Index (CPI).
    • CPI = BCWP / ACWP
    • If the CPI is greater than 1, the project is under budget. If it is less than 1, the project is over budget.

Method 7 of 7

  1. 1
    Compute the budgeted cost for the entire project by adding the BCWS for all project tasks. The resulting total is known as the "Budget at Completion" (BAC).
  2. 2
    There are 2 methods of estimating total cost of a project at its completion ("Estimate at Completion" or EAC). You should use the method more appropriate for the specific circumstances of your project.
    • If the current cost variance is the result of something that occurred that is not likely to continue, then the BCWS for the remainder of the project is likely still valid. Subtract the Cost Variance from the Budget at Completion to estimate the total project cost at completion: EAC = BAC � CV.
    • If the current cost variance is the result of circumstances that are likely to continue (such as a higher than expected cost of labor), then divide the Budget at Completion by the Cost Performance Index to estimate the total project cost: EAC = BAC / CPI.
